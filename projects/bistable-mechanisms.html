project 08

Multibody Dynamics of Bistable Mechanisms

B.Tech Thesis · Aerospace Engineering, IIT Bombay · Guide: Prof. Amuthan A. Ramabathiran · 2020–2021

The short version: I built Python simulators for how linkage mechanisms move under forces — first with the Direct Stiffness Method (elastic links), then with Lagrangian mechanics (rigid links). I used them to simulate Theo Jansen's famous walking linkage, then built a mathematical model of bistability (a system with two stable resting states) and used it to make a parallelogram mechanism that behaves like a tiny mechanical "neuron" — one that adds two numbers using nothing but geometry, springs, and hinges.

fun findings

Things That Surprised Me

  • A simple hinged parallelogram has infinite equilibrium shapes — it just flops into whatever skew you last pushed it to. Bracing it with one diagonal link kills that extra freedom entirely, and it snaps back to its original shape every time.
  • With zero damping, the simulation oscillated forever at constant amplitude, exactly as the math predicted — a satisfying sanity check that the solver conserved energy correctly before I added any damping.
  • A single parallelogram with a torsional spring will "pick a side" — settling at either +θ₀ or −θ₀ depending on which way it's nudged. That's a bistable switch made entirely of bars and a spring.
  • Chaining two of those switches together and tuning their spring stiffnesses as weights produced a working mechanical adder: it computed 0.4·A + 0.6·B using nothing electronic — just geometry and stored elastic energy.
  • The adder wasn't perfect — the Lagrange multipliers used to enforce the closed-loop constraint introduced small tracking errors, visible as a stray point in the output plots. A good reminder that constrained multibody solvers need care at the joints.
  • Simulated the Jansen linkage — the same 11-bar mechanism behind Theo Jansen's kinetic Strandbeesten sculptures — and traced the exact path its "foot" carves through the air.

why it matters

Implications

Computing without electronics

The adder performs real arithmetic using only stored elastic energy and geometry — relevant wherever electronics are impractical: extreme temperatures, radiation, or fully passive/soft robotic systems.

Mechanical metamaterials

Bistable linkage units are a building block for structures whose behavior comes from their geometry rather than their material — programmable, reconfigurable, shape-morphing designs like auxetic or deployable structures.

A direct line to my day job

Constrained rigid-body kinematics, Jacobians, closed-loop solving — the exact same problem class as the scenegraph and rigid-body transforms I now work on for exploded views in production CAD software.

further extension

Where This Could Go

  • Tighten the closed-loop constraint solver to remove the small Lagrange-multiplier tracking error, for cleaner logic-gate outputs.
  • Validate the simulated bistable "neuron" against a physically fabricated model.
  • Extend the proportional adder toward full mechanical logic gates (AND / OR / NOT) built from linked bistable units — a step toward purely mechanical computation.
  • Chain many bistable units into 2D/3D networks to realize programmable, shape-morphing mechanical metamaterials.
