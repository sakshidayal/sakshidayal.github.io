project 08

Multibody Dynamics of Bistable Mechanisms

B.Tech Thesis · Aerospace Engineering, IIT Bombay · Guide: Prof. Amuthan A. Ramabathiran · 2020–2021

Three linked ideas, in order: a mechanism that walks on its own geometry, a bistable unit that always settles into one of two shapes, and a mechanical adder built by wiring two of those units together — logic performed with no electronics at all.

the mechanism

A Leg That Walks Itself

Turn one crank in a circle, and a simple four-bar linkage traces this looping path with its foot — stepping forward, lifting, and resetting, forever. This is the same principle behind Theo Jansen's Strandbeesten and the Jansen linkage I simulated for the thesis: one motion in, a walking gait out.

bistability

A Switch With Two Homes

A hinged mechanism with a biasing spring won't rest in the middle — it settles fully to one side or the other, +θ₀ or −θ₀, and stays there until pushed hard enough to flip. That's the whole idea behind a bistable mechanism: two stable shapes, one unstable tipping point between them.

mechanical logic

Two Switches, One Adder

Wire two bistable units — A and B — into a shared linkage, and their spring stiffnesses become weights. The output position is literally 0.4·A + 0.6·B, computed by geometry alone. This is the mechanical "neuron" from the thesis, and a small step toward mechanical logic gates.

further extension

From Switches to Gates

The same two switches, gated at a threshold instead of weighted, behave like real logic gates — shown here beside their standard schematic symbols. This wasn't built in the thesis, but it's the natural next step it points toward: purely mechanical AND, OR, and NOT.

Built with Direct Stiffness Method and Lagrangian mechanics solvers in Python. The same constrained rigid-body kinematics shows up in my day job now — scenegraphs and rigid-body transforms for exploded views in production CAD software.
